Pasalubong

Yesterday, I gave a way a bunch of pasalubong, snacks, and souvenirs to my friends, family, and co-workers after a much needed break in an island province.

I was burning out really bad. I was seeing too many things that weren't there. And frankly, my health was secretly on a decline. Coughs, colds, aches - all were constant. It was only during Holy Week of the year xxxx where I was able to find the strength to recover.

We stayed at a small private resort near the pier, courtesy of my rich and very generous uncle, who invited extended family (5 different families) to stay over at their province. We felt the warmth of family and companionship within ourselves - and it caused time to tick faster for the lot of us. It felt like 5 days went by in a single afternoon - and when we left, we felt no sadness, only love. The best kind - with no real attachment, with the certainty that we'll meet again someday, one way or another.

Our family was always the generous and supportive kind - so much so that we tend to take kindesses for granted. My grandfather and his siblings especially. My clan especially. It's not only engrained in our culture, but in our blood as Chinese. Or Chinoys, at the very least.

I came to school carrying a blue eco bag filled with peanut biscuits, sandwich spreads, keychains, and fridge magnets and I started giving it to everyone. My only regret is not having bought more. I can't remember how much it cost, but I didn't need to, because the money to buy it wasn't mine to begin with - it was my grandfathers.

I was fully capable of buying pasalubong myself, but when I brought up the idea to my grandfather, he brought out two 1000-Peso bills and gave it to me. It was more than enough. In our family, we always have more than enough. The cup is never empty. The love and kindness flows out like water down a stream. It goes to the sea, but it always comes back - because how on earth would the stream flow if it didn't have a source of water to begin with?

Beer and karaoke shared with family. Latest updates. Jokes, laughs, and even interrogations that went embarassingly funny. 

"Do you have a girlfiend yet? What have you been up to? How much money to build a website?"

These are the questions that I get bombarded with whenever I get in touch with my distant relatives after a time. I think this applies to everyone.

What I noticed that helps is to remember that they're family. Yes, they may be direct. Yes, they may be old. But at the end of they day, they'll always look out for you. And if you tell them what you want, they'll move heaven and earth for you to get it - even if they don't fully understand why.

In this case, for people like them, love isn't a choice. It's automatic. A snap of a finger. The beat of a heart. A warm look. A face full of creases made noticeable when your lips press into a smile.

Keep giving.

The Steward.
