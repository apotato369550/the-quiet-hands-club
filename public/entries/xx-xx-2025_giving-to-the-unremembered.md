Giving to the Unremembered

When we think of people we don't remember, we think of the people who were lost to time. Their stories untold, their graves forgotten, and their names erased. But what if I told you that some of these people were a short commute away?

Last year, around Christmas time, I went to a recovery facility - or in other words - a rehab facility for drug addicts, the mentally ill, and mendicants.

There's a reason why I believe in miracles. Because I believe that it's a miracle that after 2 years, I'd return to the facility, not as an inpatient, but as someone who remembered.

I suffered a manic psychotic episode in 2023, and I was admitted to the facility and was placed in care for 3 months. In and out. I was barely lucid during the first 2 weeks, but as soon as I realized what I had done to myself, my world came crashing down. I was tired because of the meds, I was lonely because I had no friends. I gained weight, I became ugly - a shell of the athletic self that I had been. My hobbies I could no longer access nor had the motivation to get back into. My passion - computers - was something I resented. My scholarship, something that would have eased the burden on me and my family, in jeopardy.

It felt like the entire world was conspiring against me. My mind, my heart, my eyes, my body - everything. Yet somehow, I marched forward.

I keep telling other people my story, but words can only do so much to - not just describe - but capture the entire experience and journey back to baseline.

I usually get met with "Woah!" or something more basic, which is why I don't really talk to people about it as much, because does the past really matter anyways?

The only evidence of the past is within our minds and what's in front of us right now.

It's a miracle.

It truly is a miracle.

I'm aiming for another outreach event where we'll raise donations for them. The facility itself, although a private and government accredited institution, felt a lot like a cross between a prison and a college dorm. Makes me wonder what actual prison is like in this country.

I was going through a harsh rejection when I did the outreach, and revisiting the facility after 2 years taught me that a lot of the time, what we experience as struggle, is nothing compared to other people. Empathy. To walk in another's shoes. To see what they see, feel what they feel - and to only be able to imagine what they're thinking of right now.

Slippers, cake, and juice was what I gave. And in return, I got hugs, smiles, thank you's, and a heart that felt ten times lighter.

It takes a village to raise a child. If I wrote down the things that people have done good for me in order for me to recover; like my family, my life coach, my only friends at the time, and even some of the people I met in the facility, this essay would be a lot longer.

The love, patience, and faith they had invested into me; time may pass on, but I will never forget those who helped me when I went astray. 

Walking that road, step by step,

The Steward.
